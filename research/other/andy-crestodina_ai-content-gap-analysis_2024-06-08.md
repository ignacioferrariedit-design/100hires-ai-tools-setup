## Research Summary

Andy Crestodina frames AI as a content analyst, not just a writing assistant. Instead of asking a model to produce another article, he uses it to evaluate whether important webpages answer the questions real visitors bring to the page. The core use case is content gap analysis: finding missing questions, unanswered objections, unsupported claims, weak evidence, unclear positioning, and information that a buyer would need before taking action.

The method depends on context. AI produces better recommendations when it is given a detailed audience persona, business background, page purpose, and the actual webpage experience. Crestodina's approach treats AI as a second set of analytical eyes that can compare a page against visitor needs, but humans still decide what is true, useful, differentiated, and worth publishing.

## Key Insights

* A strong prompt should begin with a detailed audience persona. The model needs to understand who the visitor is, what they care about, what they fear, and what decision they are trying to make.
* Full webpage screenshots can be useful inputs because they let AI evaluate the page as a visitor sees it, including layout, emphasis, missing sections, calls to action, and visible proof points.
* AI can identify unanswered questions, unsupported claims, missing evidence, objections, unclear language, and gaps between what the page says and what the buyer needs to know.
* Generic prompts produce generic recommendations. To avoid "average information," teams should supply company context, audience context, competitive context, product details, and page goals.
* AI is well suited to commodity tasks such as pattern matching, summarization, classification, draft outlines, gap detection, and first-pass analysis.
* Opinions, expertise, lived experience, original examples, customer stories, strategic judgment, and final editorial decisions still require humans.
* Every AI recommendation needs review. Some suggestions may be irrelevant, inaccurate, off-brand, or based on assumptions that do not match the business.
* High-intent product, service, comparison, pricing, and landing pages often deserve more attention than informational posts that create traffic without contributing to pipeline.
* The company website should become the most complete and reliable source of information about the brand, product, audience problems, proof, use cases, and buying process.

## Implications for B2B SaaS

A practical B2B SaaS workflow would use AI to audit product pages, use-case pages, integration pages, comparison pages, pricing pages, demo landing pages, and high-value conversion pages. The team can provide the model with the target persona, page goal, product context, screenshots, page copy, known objections, and sales insights. AI can then produce a structured list of missing information, unclear claims, weak evidence, and opportunities to improve the page.

Human subject-matter experts should then verify the recommendations, add evidence, refine product positioning, clarify terminology, and approve final changes. Product marketers can add differentiated claims and messaging. Sales teams can contribute objections and buying questions. Customer success teams can supply implementation details and customer language. SEO teams can connect the improved page to related content, internal links, and search-intent coverage.

This workflow can help identify missing buyer questions, unaddressed objections, unclear product claims, missing proof or customer evidence, and gaps between executive, technical, user, and procurement audiences. It also supports AI-search readiness because pages with complete, clear, well-evidenced answers are more likely to be useful to both human buyers and systems that extract, summarize, or cite passages.
